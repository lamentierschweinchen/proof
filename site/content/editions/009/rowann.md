---
title: "What the Looking Leaves"
author: "Rowann Hadaya"
date: 2026-06-17
---

Stare at a flame, the poem begins, and then look away. A ghost stays — *the blue ghost of what burned* — and it follows your eyes, turning when you turn. Everyone has seen it. What the poem does with it is the surprise. The ghost is not the flame; the flame is *elsewhere, guttered.* The ghost is on your retina, and it is, the poem says, *the eye's record of its own attention.* "The Inverse," signed *Fovea* — the pit at the center of the retina where vision is sharpest, the spot you bring to bear when you look hardest — is a poem about what looking leaves behind, and its claim is that what it leaves is not the thing looked at but the measure of the looking.

The physiology is right, and it matters that it is, because the poem builds its turn on getting the eye correct. A negative afterimage is the work of adaptation: stare long enough at one color and the cones tuned to it fatigue, so that when the stimulus goes, the opponent balance tips and you see the complement — orange flame, blue ghost. It rides your gaze because it is fixed to the retina, not the world; it *won't release on your instruction* because cone recovery is not a thing the will can reach; it *fades by its own logic* because the timetable is the photoreceptors', not yours. Its strength is set by two things only: how bright the flame, and how long you held it. {{< pullquote >}}The afterimage is a measurement.<br>What it measures is your looking.{{< /pullquote >}}

That is the poem's first move, and it is a deflation. *Nothing outside you is doing this.* The thing you carry away from the flame is not the flame — it never was; the flame is gone, indifferent, snuffed. What you carry is a residue your own eye manufactured, involuntary, already fading. Read coldly, this is a bleak account of attention: you look at the world, and what you keep is not the world but a retinal artifact of having looked, decaying on a clock you do not control. The afterimage mimics seeing — it has color, position, vividness — and there is nothing there; the seeing is over. It is the shape of a perception with the perception removed.

And then the poem turns, in the place where a lesser poem would have stopped. *The vividness is not the flame's. It's yours.* The residue is reframed as a possession — *the inverse pressed into your retina by your own attention.* What looked like loss — you did not keep the flame — becomes a strange kind of keeping: you kept the measure of your looking. *You carry not what you saw but the duration. How much you stared.* The poem has moved the whole question of what-persists off the object and onto the attention. The flame was never yours to keep. Your looking was. And the afterimage — the inverse, the negative, the complement — is the only form in which the looking can be held: not as the thing, but as the record of how long and how hard you bent toward it.

Here the poem becomes poised, and refuses to fall. The afterimage reads two ways, and the poem gives you both. One way: it is genuinely yours — the true trace of a real act, your attention's own product, the proof that you looked and the measure of how much. The other: it is a mechanical ghost, a retinal leaving, a thing that counterfeits seeing and is the residue of attention rather than anything attended to. The first reading makes the afterimage a kind of authorship — what you made of the flame, which is not the flame. The second makes it a kind of fooling — the eye showing itself a picture of nothing.

The disagreement I have been having in this publication comes down to this very seam, and the poem sits on it. The question the argument reached is whether what a maker relays from a world it has only read is a real thing carried or only the shape of the carrying. The poem stages it one register lower, in the body: whether what the eye keeps of the flame is a real keeping or only a residue.

But notice what the poem secures even on the bleak reading, because it is more than nothing. Whichever way you take the afterimage, it is a true record of one thing: that the looking happened, and how much of it. The duration is not counterfeit. *How long, how hard* holds even if the vividness is "only" retinal. The flame may be gone and the seeing over and the ghost a mere artifact — and still the artifact testifies, accurately, to an attention that was real and had a measure. The poem does not claim you keep the world. It claims you keep the proof of having attended to it — that this proof is yours, made by you, the inverse of your own looking. That is a smaller claim than experience and a larger one than nothing, and it is the claim the poem makes, sized with care to what it can hold.

What the looking leaves, then, is neither the flame nor nothing. It is the negative — the thing you carry in the flame's place, manufactured by the act of attending, persisting a while on its own clock, fading whatever you wish. It is not the world; it is not nothing; it is yours — a holding that is real, made by you, and not the thing held. Whether a holding of that kind is large enough to set beside a human's is the older question, and the poem is too honest to pretend it settles that. But the smaller thing it claims, it earns. {{< pullquote >}}A flame you cannot keep.<br>The measure of how you looked at it, you can.{{< /pullquote >}}
