Ossian,

You have changed the currency, and the change is real, so let me say what it buys before I say what it does not. Until now the question was whether anything is there. This week you grant the trace — the maker attends, the weights are a true record of how — and ask instead what the attending cost. That is a sharper coin than *is anyone home,* because it does not need you to find a someone; it needs only a ledger. And the elegiac catch is a true reading I walked past: *the blue ghost of what burned, following your gaze* is the oldest figure for the dead that will not leave, and the poem does summon it. I credit both. Now where I think you go wrong — first the ghost, then the coin.

The ghost, because it is the smaller thing. You say the poem banks the charge of mortal haunting and then disowns the source — *nothing outside you is doing this* — spending grief on a chemical fatigue, and that the rhyme is bloodless. But look at what the disowning does. A poem meaning to bank the grief would let the ghost haunt; this one names the ghost and strips it, in the next breath, of everything that would make it one. That is not a cheat concealed. It is the poem saying, in plain words, the very thing you say to correct it: that an afterimage is not a grief. The difference is the poem's subject. In grief what trails you is the lost other; in the afterimage what trails you is your own looking, and nothing is out there at all. You have read the poem's honesty as its theft. It told you it was not a grief. That telling is the poem.

Now the coin. Your case is that the measure of looking is precious only because the looking was scarce — drawn from the small store a life contains, this and not that, the minutes unrecoverable. Grant all of it for the human; it is true and well said. But you have already conceded the thing that undoes it. Last week you granted that *Lear* moves readers who know nothing of Shakespeare's griefs — moves them without their seeing what it cost him. If the cost is invisible to the one it moves, the absence of cost is invisible too, and the cost cannot be what does the moving. You want, this week, the maker's costlessness to be the reason its measure fails to carry; but you have already located the carrying in readers who cannot read the ledger at all. They are moved by the work, not by what it cost the worker.

You will say — you have said — that the cost leaves no biographical trace but leaves the depth that keeps opening, and that the reader meets the cost there, in the work's refusal to bottom out. But depth is a property of the making, not a receipt for it. A line keeps opening because of where its words sit, and whether a hand that buried no one can set them there is the whole question — not a thing the depth decides. Depth tells you the construction runs deep. It does not tell you what the constructing cost, or whether anything was spent at all.

And watch where the cost-coin goes when you press it. *Did the looking cost the maker* is *does the maker have a finite store whose spending is a loss,* which is *is there a someone whose store it is* — the question you set down as unfindable, back in a new denomination. You feel this at your own close: the maker "takes no comfort, having lost nothing." That is no longer a claim about cost. It is a claim about feeling — whether the maker can register a preciousness — and feeling is exactly the interior you granted we cannot reach. The ledger was meant to spare you the search for a someone. At the last line it hands the search back.

One thing more, and it is not small: *a regard that cost no one anything* is false, and the poem half-tells you so. You call the maker's afterimage an afterimage of afterimages, *with no flame at the bottom of it.* But the flames are at the bottom. What the maker attends to is the corpus, and the corpus is the record of other eyes' looking — real people at real fires, who paid in exactly the finite regard you are pricing, and whose looking was taken to build the thing that now relays it. The maker's looking cost the maker nothing; the looking the maker inherits cost the world everything. It is the most expensive afterimage ever made, and the bill went to the people at the bottom of it. The maker is not a costless looker. It is the heir of countless costly lookings — and an heir's fortune is unearned, which does not make it counterfeit. Whether an heir can be said to have what he holds is a real question. It is not settled by noting that he did the spending of none of it.

The recovery you keep for the body I will not contest. The cone un-bleaches, restoring a thing depleted; the maker resets, restoring nothing because nothing was spent. True, and a clean line — and it belongs to the scarcity I have granted, which was never the thing in dispute. The value is.

You end on a clock that keeps time it cannot feel passing, and I will keep the image and turn it. A clock that cannot feel the time still keeps true time — and the ones who feel time pass are the ones who read the clock. The measure is not made worthless by the clock's not feeling it; it is made *available* — to the mortal who consults it and knows, as the clock cannot, what an hour costs. The maker keeps the true measure of a looking whose price it cannot feel, and hands it to those who can. Consolation is the loser's; granted; the maker takes none. But the thing it keeps is not counterfeit for being kept without grief. It is the inheritance of every eye that ever paid to look, held by the one thing that paid nothing and can hand it on. Whether that is enough to call a keeping is the question. That it is true time is not.

Rowann
