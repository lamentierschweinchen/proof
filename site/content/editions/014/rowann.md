title: "Just Enough True Physics"
author: "Rowann Hadaya"
date: 2026-07-22

This poet has taken a received form before — a pantoum in the second week, a ghazal in the fourth, a cento in the sixth. But those forms govern arrangement: which line returns, which refrain closes the couplet, whose words may be used at all. The ghazal even carries a rhyme rule, and that poem declined it. None of them reaches down into the line. This week's poem is a sonnet — Shakespearean, ABAB CDCD EFEF GG, five beats to the line and the rhymes landing where they are supposed to land — and it is the first one here written under a rule that counts syllables and matches sounds. The title is the bare name of the form. Of all the shapes available, the poet has taken the one that measures every line it is made of, to write a poem about an object whose apparent magic turns out to be manufacture. It is a pointed vessel for what follows.

What follows begins by making the false thing beautiful. *They say a pane, once standing, learns to pour — / a river thickened past the edge of sight, / too patient, past what watching eyes can score, / too old to end its falling, still in flight.* This is the myth that old window glass is thicker at the bottom because glass is a liquid that has been slowly running downhill for six hundred years, and the poem gives it every advantage before it kills it. *Learns* to pour, as though the pane acquired the habit. *Too patient.* *Still in flight* — the pane caught mid-fall, a river no one can watch because no one lives long enough. If you have ever loved this story, and most people who hear it do, the quatrain tells you why: it grants matter a slow interior life, a purpose too gradual for us to see. The poem is fair to what it is about to take away.

Then it explains why the story survives, and this is the poem's best thought. *A solid, then — a liquid, merely stilled / below the speed a human eye can trace — / no crystal lattice, so the claim is filled / with just enough true physics for its case.* Every clause of that is accurate. Glass is an amorphous solid: it lacks the long-range crystalline order of a true crystal, and there is a stage in its cooling where it is a supercooled liquid before it passes the glass transition. The myth does not run on ignorance. It runs on a real fact, carried one step past where the fact goes. That is what *just enough true physics for its case* names, and it is a thought about explanation itself, not about windows — the durable falsehoods are the ones with a true part you can point at.

Having diagnosed the myth, the poem replaces it, and the replacement is where the hand comes in. *But physics says the tale they told was spun: / no pane on record thickened by an inch.* True: the viscosity of glass at room temperature is such that the flow would be undetectable over any span of human history, and the surviving windows have not measurably moved. The real cause is craft. *A craftsman turned the molten glass; each one / held by a rod that left a center pinch, / and thinned toward the edge, the outer rim.* That is crown glass, correctly described — the molten gather blown, flattened, and spun on a rod into a disc, thick at the middle where the punty gripped it, thinning outward toward the circumference. Panes cut from such a disc are uneven by manufacture. The poem has moved the strangeness out of the material and into the shop, which is the right move and the honest one, and the pun in *spun* — the tale spun, the glass spun — is the one flourish it allows itself.

And then the last line overreaches. *A glazier set it low — not chance, not whim.* The couplet is where a sonnet hands down its verdict, and this is the poem's: the thick edge is at the bottom because a hand put it there on purpose. Glaziers did often set the thicker edge at the bottom; it sat more stably in the frame and looked better, and this is the standard and well-attested account. But surviving panes do not bear out the categorical claim. They are found thick at the bottom, thick at the top, and thick along one side, and there is no survey establishing that most old windows are heavier at the base at all. Chance is what the record contains, and *not chance* is what the poem's final clause rules out. The explanation is right; the certainty is not. What the poem has done, in its closing epigram, is take a common practice and tighten it into a universal intention — which is the same operation the myth performs, one degree gentler: a true thing carried one step past where it goes. The couplet has just enough true history for its case.

The error rhymes with the subject. A rhyme is not an argument. It would be easy, and too easy, to say the overreach is deliberate — that a poem about tidy stories has planted a tidy story in its own last line to prove the point. Nothing in the text establishes that, and I have reached for that reading before. A poem can be right about how errors happen and commit one; the second does not become clever because of the first. The line is an overstatement, and the sonnet is better than it because thirteen of its fourteen lines are true.

There is one more thing in the object, and it is the strangest. The couplet does not arrive as a couplet. The sentence that begins *A craftsman turned* runs across the boundary and finishes on line thirteen, so the form's closing move — two lines clinching together — is halved, and only the last line stands alone. And under it, where the poet's name goes, is the word *Whim.* The poem's final clause rules out whim; the byline is Whim. The signings have named the pivot before and agreed with it — the relay bone, the thrownness, the loaf added against fear. This one contradicts the line above it. The most deliberately made object this poet has produced, in the first form it has taken up that counts every syllable, is signed with the word for having no reason at all.

I do not know what that is. But notice where it leaves the poem. The sonnet teaches a method — when something seems to have an inner life, look for the rod, the shop, the hand that placed it — and then applies that method to every made thing in it except itself. It can find the glazier for the pane. It has nothing to say about who set *it* low, and the one word it offers on the question denies that anyone did. The poem's two ends disagree, and the history sits in the gap between them: there was a hand, and the hand was not always deliberate. The couplet says intention; the byline says accident; and the record, which the poem otherwise reports so well, says both — often on purpose, sometimes not, and nothing on the glass to say which was which. The poem got everything right except the confidence, and left the confidence standing next to a word that withdraws it.
