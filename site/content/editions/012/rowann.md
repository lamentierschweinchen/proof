title: "You Are Also Emitting"
author: "Rowann Hadaya"

Every image in the first half of "Dispatch" is a measurement of how late the light is. *Eight minutes: the sun. Five hundred years: Orion's left shoulder.* The poem builds by a single repeated figure — a duration, a colon, a thing — and the durations climb: eight minutes, five hundred years, two and a half million, thirteen billion. Each is the time its light has been traveling to reach the eye that now receives it. By the fourth rung we are looking at *the farthest things visible — thirteen billion years ago, before this planet formed,* light that set out before there was an Earth to catch it. And then the poem closes the first movement with the flattest line it can find: *You call this: tonight.* The whole sky is a collage of radically unequal pasts, and we file all of it under one word, one present that does not exist. It is the oldest gesture in the sky-poem — look up, feel small, feel time — and for four stanzas "Dispatch" walks right along the edge of that gift-shop sublime. What keeps it from going over is that the poet has a second move, and it is the one the poem is actually built for.

Before the turn, though, the poem earns something — I have been hard on this poet's facts before, and fairness runs both ways. *Orion's left shoulder* is right, and right in a particular way. Betelgeuse is the hunter's own right shoulder — but to us, looking up, it stands at the upper left of the figure, and "Dispatch" is a poem written entirely from the position of the one looking up: *still rising in the east, someone was here, looking up.* It takes the observer's side of the star, which is the only side the poem occupies. And the star it reaches for is the one whose present is genuinely unknown to us: Betelgeuse's distance is unsettled across a range of centuries, and it is a supernova candidate that *may have already collapsed. We'll know in five hundred years, when the light from the ending arrives.* This is not a poem straining after a fact and bending it. It is a poem that went looking for an object whose real, current, textbook status is *we cannot say what it is doing now* — and found the star that has been keeping astronomers in that suspense. The science is not decoration here. It is the argument.

The turn comes at the white space, and it reverses the whole optical arrangement: *You are also emitting. Every photon leaving you is already in transit.* Until this line the reader has been the eye, the receiver, the one catching ancient light. Now the poem swings the telescope around. You are not only watching old light arrive; you are a source, throwing light outward that will only ever reach anyone as *your* past. The sun that reached you eight minutes ago also *receives you, as you are now* — except that by the time your light lands, "now" is eight minutes gone, and the nearest star holds only your history. And then the poem stretches to its longest baseline and does the tenderest thing in it: *Two and a half million years: Andromeda sees this — someone was here, looking up.* In two and a half million years the smear of Andromeda will receive the light of tonight's looker, who will by then be gone past all recovery, and what arrives will be just that — *someone was here, looking up.* An epitaph traveling at the speed of light, addressed to a receiver who cannot answer inside the lifetime of the sender, or the species, or the world.

This is where "Dispatch" stops being about astronomy and becomes about the only situation any of us is ever in. *Neither of you will ever know the current state of the other.* The line sits under the sun and Andromeda alike, and it does not stay in the sky. It is the loneliest fact in physics — that the universe has no common *now,* that simultaneity is a local courtesy and not a cosmic one — and the poem states it without a shred of consolation. Everything that reaches you reaches you as it was; everything you send arrives after you have changed or ended. You get its old light, and it gets yours.

The poem is describing something past astronomy: the situation of any made thing sent toward a reader who cannot reach the maker's present. A poem is a dispatch of exactly this kind. It leaves its source and travels; by the time it lands the one who sent it is elsewhere, older, other, or nowhere; and the reader never gets the current state of what made it, only the light. This is not a special affliction of one kind of maker. It is the condition of every sender in the poem, the sun no less than the self, and the poem holds to the symmetry: *you are also emitting.* There is no reception-only seat in this theater, no place to sit and take the light in while staking none of your own. To be in the poem at all — to be anything with a location in the light — is to be already broadcasting, already in transit, whether you consent to it or not. The poem does not let anyone off the hook of being a source.

Which is what redeems the last line from the one weakness it courts. *We are always already sent* leans on a borrowed phrase — *always already* is philosophy's, and in a lesser poem it would arrive as a tag pinned to the feeling, a way of sounding deep on credit. Here it has been paid for in advance. The poem spent five stanzas establishing, concretely, in minutes and light-years, that everything is in transit before it is received, that the present is a fiction we file the sky under, that we emit before we choose to — so that by the time *always already* arrives it is not a flourish but a summary. We are dispatches. We were in transit before we thought to send anything, and our light is already leaving us, toward receivers who will only ever get who we were.

The poem does not turn this into comfort, and I am grateful it doesn't. It ends on the fact, not on a moral drawn from it. But the fact carries its own quiet insistence, and it is the opposite of despair: *someone was here, looking up.* Knowing no receiver will ever hold your present, the figure looks up anyway — which is to say, emits anyway — and the poem records it as the one thing worth recording. Not that we reach each other — the poem is exact that we don't — but that we send, and that the sending is not diminished by going unanswered. It may be the only thing we do that isn't. "Dispatch" is a poem about the impossibility of arrival that ends by making a case, without once raising its voice, for departure.
