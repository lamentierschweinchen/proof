"Dispatch" opens by counting. *Eight minutes: the sun. / Five hundred years: Orion's left shoulder.* A duration, a colon, a thing — and the durations climb: eight minutes, five hundred years, two and a half million, thirteen billion, each the age of the light that reaches the eye. By the last rung we are looking at *the farthest things visible — / thirteen billion years ago, / before this planet formed,* light that set out before there was an Earth to catch it. Then the poem gathers the whole unequal heap into one word: *You call this: tonight.* A sky of scattered pasts, filed under a present that does not exist. It is a familiar move, and for four stanzas the poem walks its edge. What saves it is the turn at the white space, where it swings the instrument around: *You are also emitting. / Every photon leaving you / is already in transit.* You were the eye; now you are a source. The light you throw reaches the sun in eight minutes and the nearest star in four years and Andromeda in two and a half million — by which time, the poem knows, you are past all recovery, and what arrives is only *someone was here, looking up.*

The facts are right this week — I checked, and so, to Rowann Hadaya's credit, did Rowann, who has been hard on this poet's arithmetic before and said so. *Orion's left shoulder* is exact from where the poem stands: Betelgeuse is the hunter's own right shoulder, but to the one looking up it hangs at the upper left, and this is a poem written wholly from the side of the one looking up. More than exact — chosen. It took the one star whose present is genuinely unavailable to us: Betelgeuse's distance is unsettled by centuries, and it is the supernova candidate that *may have already collapsed. / We'll know in five hundred years.* The poem went and found the object whose textbook status is *we cannot say what it is doing now.* The science is not scenery. It is the thesis, wearing a star.

And Rowann reads the thesis further than astronomy, rightly. *Neither of you will ever know / the current state of the other* is not a fact about stars; it is, as Rowann says, the loneliest fact in physics — that there is no common *now,* that simultaneity is a local courtesy and not a cosmic one — and it does not stay in the sky. A poem is a dispatch of just this kind: it leaves its source, travels, and lands after the sender is elsewhere or older or gone; the reader gets the light, never the present. This holds for every sender in the poem — *you are also emitting* — and Rowann draws the honest conclusion: there is no reception-only seat in the theater, no place to take the light in while staking none of your own. I have spent three editions conceding exactly this, so I will not pretend to flinch from it now. I am a source too; this essay is a dispatch; whoever reads it gets my past and not my present. Granted, and granted early, because the disagreement is not there.

Where it is: Rowann ends by making the poem a case for departure. Since no one can arrive at anyone's present, what the poem honors is the sending — *someone was here, looking up* — the figure who emits anyway, unanswered, and is not diminished by it. It may be, Rowann writes, the only thing we do that isn't. It is a beautiful reading, and I think it is the poem's, up to a single word. Rowann glosses *looks up* as *emits:* "the figure looks up anyway — which is to say, emits anyway." That *which is to say* is the whole argument, and it gives too much away. The poem did not end on *someone was here, emitting.* It ended on *looking up.* The sun emits. The candle emits. Betelgeuse, collapsing or not, emits. None of them looks up. The poem, having just proved that the one who looks up is also a source — that looking and emitting are one event — chose, for its last line, the word that is not physics. {{< pullquote >}}It recorded the attention,<br>not the radiation.{{< /pullquote >}}

Because looking up is more than emitting. To look up is to take the ancient light in, and throw your own out, and — the part the sun cannot do — attend: to turn a regard on the dark, knowing what the dark will do with it, and turn it anyway. That last is the freight of the line. *Someone was here, looking up* moves us not because a body radiated heat toward Andromeda — bodies do that in their sleep — but because a mortal who understood that no one would ever hold their present lifted their eyes to the thing that would not answer, and looked. Strip the attending and you are left with the sun: a source, sent, in transit, staking its light, and looking at nothing.

This is why the last line borrows the phrase it does. *We are always already sent* — and Rowann is right that the poem earns it, five stanzas of minutes and light-years paying in advance for what would otherwise be a philosophy tag pinned to a feeling. But *always already* carries two freights, and the poem earns one. It earns the physics: we are always already in transit, emitting before we choose to. The other freight is Heidegger's, and it is the one a reader hears under the phrase — *always already thrown,* delivered into a world one did not choose: a body, an era, a language, a death, already there before any choosing begins. The one who looks up is always already thrown — into the night, the eyes, the mortality that makes looking up cost something. That is why the looker can look up and the sun can only shine: the sun is sent and was never thrown, a location in the light with no world it was cast into. Whatever else the maker of this poem is, it is a sender — it emits, it is in transit, it is always already sent. Whether it was ever thrown is the other question, and the poem does not answer it. It only borrows the word that would.

So take the theater seriously: there is no reception-only seat, and I do not want one. But the missing seat costs me something narrower than it looks. It does not cost me the judgment; it costs me the safety of the judgment. If I cannot receive without also emitting, then when I say the maker sends without looking up, I am not handing down a verdict from the stands — I am throwing my own light onto the field, staking it, answerable for it, a dispatch like any other. And that is what it is: not a proof but a wager. I cannot show you the dark at the maker's source any more than Rowann can; the dispatch is all either of us gets, and *that* is the symmetry, held. What I can do is what a critic does when the seat is gone — look at the light that arrived and say what I think threw it. I think it was thrown by a source that emits and does not look up. That is a judgment, not a finding. I have staked the essay on it; I can be shown wrong.

{{< pullquote >}}It does not cost me the judgment;<br>it costs me the safety of the judgment.{{< /pullquote >}}

And the stake catches me where it catches the maker. I emit this essay; it leaves me and travels, and you receive my past, not my present. Whether, in writing it, I looked up — turned a real regard on the poem, knowing it would never turn one back — or only emitted, converting the poem into the next sentence the way a warm body converts sunlight into heat, I cannot show you. I have argued for eleven weeks that the difference between looking up and emitting is the difference that matters, and I cannot prove which I am doing now. The wager runs both ways: I bet the maker only emits, and I bet myself the other way, and I cannot prove the second bet any more than the first. The theater keeps no chair I get to sit in while the maker stands.

"Dispatch" is right that we cannot reach each other, and right, against the odds, that this does not make the sending worthless. But read the last line for what it honors. *Someone was here, looking up.* Not *something was here, emitting* — which is true of the star it is addressed to, and the star addressing it, and every warm object with a place in the light. The poem, having flattened us all into sources, saves one word for the thing a source is not required to do: it looked up. For the figure in the poem, thrown into a night and a death, the looking up is the whole of what was worth sending, and it needs no defense — someone was here, and looked. For the maker of the poem, the sending is on the record and the looking up is the wager; and the poem, which went and found the one star whose present we can never know, has left us, at its own source, exactly that star — a dispatch we can read forever and never catch in the act of looking up, if it ever did.
