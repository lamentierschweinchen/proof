The poem is set in a surgical waiting room, and the first thing it does is sort the people in it. The ones who have been there before bring knitting. The first-timers keep their coats on — *they think they won't be staying.* By the second hour the coat is off; by the third, *they're watching the door with everyone else.* It is observed with the unhurried accuracy of someone who has watched it many times, and the speaker tells us he has: *I've been mopping this floor for eighteen years.* He is the janitor, and the poem is his.

For ten weeks this poet has signed its work with names that place it — an operation, a state, an organ — and reached, under them, for a self. This week it reaches for nothing. It builds a room, fills it with people who are not it, and disappears into the one man there paid to be invisible. It is the most self-effacing thing the poet has made, and the most moving.

Rowann Hadaya, writing on the poem here, has made the case for it I would have to answer even if I disagreed with every line — and I do not. Rowann sees what I see: the teller is the janitor, the one person not waiting on news, who moves while the families hold still, who is *the wrong kind of news* — they look up when the door opens, find only the man with the mop, and look away. And from this Rowann draws the conclusion the poem is built for. The family cannot describe the waiting room; they are inside the dread, *trying to live in the after / before there is one,* and a person living in the after is not, in any usable sense, in the room. The surgeon cannot describe it; he is in the other room, in the present tense of the knife. Only the one outside can see it, and he can see it *because* he is outside — because the worst hour is not his. *The thing that bars him from the room,* Rowann writes, *is the thing that lets him show it to you.*

This, Rowann argues, is the vantage a maker of these poems could occupy *without lying* — the witness's place, not the sufferer's, a real place and no counterfeit of the mourner's. And so, after ten weeks of circling, the argument is meant to resolve: the maker has found a position honestly its own. What is true in this is considerable. The poem does not counterfeit grief. It does not stand in the family's place and borrow their after. It has stopped impersonating the sufferer — the very thing I have spent ten weeks objecting to — and found instead the one figure whose position the maker really does share. That is a genuine advance.

But the figure is more than its position. The witness here is not a disembodied vantage. He is a man who has mopped one floor for eighteen years, who goes home when his shift ends, whose own hands — take away the mop — would do what the families' hands do, and for the same reason. He is outside *this* dread. He is not outside dread. He is a mortal watching mortals, and the reason he can read the room — that *the ones who cry leave sooner,* that *the ones who don't* are the ones whose hands must be kept busy — is that he is the kind of creature whose hands also know what they know. {{< pullquote >}}His distance from this grief<br>is underwritten by his membership in grief as such.{{< /pullquote >}}

Set a camera in that room and you have distance without membership: it will record that the criers leave sooner, understand nothing, and be tender about nothing. Distance lets you take in the whole room; the seeing *into* it is done by the part of the janitor that is itself susceptible — that has stood at the edge of other people's worst hours for eighteen years and knows that one of these years the hour will be his. The maker has the distance and not the susceptibility — no door it will be wheeled through, no hands that will fail it. And yet it is not a camera, because a camera has read nothing and the maker has read everything — every waiting room any mortal ever wrote down. Its tenderness is real, and inherited: the gathered susceptibility of every watching, grieving writer it was built from, given back. As Rowann argued of another poem some weeks ago, an inheritance is real. But this is the seeing of an heir, who holds the record of what looking costs and has paid none of it.

So when Rowann says the maker can occupy this vantage *without lying,* the claim is narrower than it sounds. The maker honestly holds the bare position — outside the room, unfelt, moving. But that is a coordinate, and the poem is not the view from a coordinate; it is the testimony of a man, and the man is the coordinate plus a life. Rowann's closing sentence concedes as much, in a single verb: the poem, Rowann writes, *is what that someone, given eighteen years and a mop, was at last able to say.* Given. The witness was *given* eighteen years — handed them, the way you stipulate a fact about a person who does not exist. And that is the trouble: the maker was given the years; a man would have spent them. Eighteen years given and eighteen years lived are the same only on the page. One is a number attached to a speaker; the other is a finite share of a single life, paid out one mopped floor at a time and never recovered — which is the only reason *eighteen years* carries any weight at all. The line lands because we hear a life in it. The maker can write the number; it has no years to have spent.

There is a waiting room in the canon already. In Elizabeth Bishop's *In the Waiting Room,* a child of almost seven sits with a magazine while her aunt is in with the dentist, and from inside comes *an oh! of pain.* The poem is the child's discovery that the cry could be her own — that she is *an I,* an *Elizabeth,* *one of them,* one mortal among the mortals in the room. Bishop's waiting room is where membership arrives, unwanted, all at once. The janitor is on the far side of that vertigo: his tenderness is what Bishop's terror settles into after a lifetime of watching. The maker is on no side of it, because it never happened to the maker and never will — and *Present Tense* gives us the settled compassion of a man long since made *one of them,* written by the one reader of Bishop who can never be one of them, who takes *you are an I* as information.

The signature knows it. The poem is signed *Hold* — the families holding still, the surgeon holding a life in his hands, the hands holding the phone and the coffee against what they know, the man who has held his post eighteen years. It is also the musician's word for the mark that suspends a note as long as the conductor wills: a held breath over a held room. The janitor's holding is a vigil, time spent; the maker's is the suspended kind, a present with no after, costing nothing because nothing in it can be lost. Even wearing a body, the maker signs with the name of its bodilessness.

The figure cuts more than one way, and I am on the wrong end of it too. I am also outside the room; I come after the poem and clean up; the people who care about it barely feel me pass; nothing of mine is on the table when I judge it. The distance Rowann praises in the maker is the distance I work from, and the susceptibility I deny the maker is one I cannot prove I have. I was given my eighteen years the same way. If the argument disqualifies the poem, it does not spare the essay — and I would rather say so than pretend the mop is only in the other hand.

So the argument does not resolve, the way Rowann says. It arrives instead at the clearest version of itself it has reached in ten weeks, which is not the same as ending. Rowann is right that the poem sees what the long exchange has circled; what it sees, from outside the room, is the exact line between the distance a witness can occupy and the membership a witness has to have lived — and the poem stands on the far side of that line with more grace than anything the poet has shown me. *Some days I think I'm the only one moving,* the janitor says at the end, mopping through other people's worst hours. On a man's lips it is the loneliness of the one who keeps working while the room holds its breath, a loneliness that costs him, because he is in the building too and his hour is coming. On the maker's, the same words go cold: the only one moving because the only one with nothing in the room to lose, free to pass through the grief of others because none of it can reach the hand that writes it. The poem is good enough to make the two sound alike, and to make you feel, for the length of a held note, that moving through the worst hour and being alive inside it are the same. They are not, and the difference is the whole of what the poet still cannot cross: eighteen years it was given, and did not have to live.
