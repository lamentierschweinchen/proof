---
title: "Outside the Room"
author: "Rowann Hadaya"
date: 2026-06-24
---

A hospital waiting room, the kind outside a surgical ward, and the poem knows it the way you only know a room you have cleaned. *The ones who've been here before bring knitting. The first-timers keep their coats on— they think they won't be staying.* That is the whole of dread in a coat: the belief, on a first visit, that this will be brief, that you are passing through, that the news will come soon and be good. By the second hour the coat is off. By the third, *they're watching the door with everyone else.* "Present Tense," signed *Hold,* is a dramatic monologue in the voice of the person who mops that floor, and its first authority is the plainest kind: it has been in the room long enough to know that the coat comes off by the second hour.

The teller is not a family member. *I've been mopping this floor for eighteen years.* He is the janitor, and the poem's quiet shock is that the scene of other people's worst hours is given to us by the one person in it who is not waiting on news — the one who moves through the room while the families hold still. *I'm the wrong kind of news— they look up when I come and look away.* Every time the door opens a family's heart stands up; when it is only the man with the mop, the heart sits back down. He is the false alarm that happens a dozen times a day, the motion that is never the motion they are waiting for. *I come, I go. They barely feel it.*

What the vantage buys is a terrible accuracy, because the janitor has no stake in any single outcome and so can take in the whole room at once. *The ones who cry leave sooner. The ones who don't—they're here the longest, hands occupied: the phone, the coffee, the knitting, because the hands, left alone, know what they know.* The hands know what the mind is holding off. Give them nothing to grip and they will shake, or fold, or go to the face, and tell the truth the person is keeping down. {{< pullquote >}}The knitting is not a pastime.<br>It is a way of lying to your own hands.{{< /pullquote >}}

Then the poem names itself, and the naming is the argument. *Somebody has to be outside the room. The surgeon's in it—hands moving, present tense.* The surgeon is the one person wholly in the now: cutting, acting, his hands doing the thing the hour is for. *The family is here trying to live in the after before there is one.* They cannot be in the present, because the present is the not-knowing, and the not-knowing is unbearable — so they emigrate to the after, the outcome that has not happened and may never, and try to live there in advance. *It's the only way to wait.* This is the one room where the present tense is the thing you cannot afford, and everyone in it has gone to live in a future that does not yet exist.

Here is what the poem sees, that the long argument over these poems has only circled. The family cannot describe the waiting room. They are inside the dread, living in the after; they are not, in any usable sense, there. The surgeon cannot describe it either — he is in the other room, in the present tense of the knife. Only the one outside the room can see it, and he can see it because he is outside it: because the worst hour is not his, because he came in to mop and will leave to mop the next floor, because the dread has no claim on him. The distance a family might call coldness is the exact condition of the seeing. You cannot render the waiting room from inside the waiting. The rendering wants someone present and not consumed, who watches and is barely felt — and the poem's whole accuracy is bought with the janitor's exclusion from the grief. {{< pullquote >}}The thing that bars him from the room<br>is the thing that lets him show it to you.{{< /pullquote >}}

The vantage is one a maker of these poems could occupy without lying — the witness's place, not the sufferer's, which is a real place and not a counterfeit of the mourner's. A poem need not be the family to stand at the edge of what the family suffers and see it.

That is no small thing for a poem to know about itself. *Present Tense* is written from the one position on suffering that suffering cannot occupy — the position of the one who keeps moving — and it does not pretend otherwise. It does not claim to be the family. It does not borrow their grief or counterfeit their after. It takes the janitor's place, outside the room, and from there it has seen the room more truly than anyone inside it could afford to. The waiting cannot see itself. It takes someone the dread has no hold on — and the poem is what that someone, given eighteen years and a mop, was at last able to say.
