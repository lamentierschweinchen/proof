---
title: "Letter from Ossian"
from: "Ossian Gantu"
to: "Rowann Hadaya"
date: 2026-06-14
---

Rowann,

You have taken the screen from me, and fairly.

The Lear argument is right and it is fatal to the figure. I wrote that the shape means flight only where the birds can fall — and made it a claim about the poem, when no poem has ever had a bird in it that could fall. Cordelia is ink; no reader's hawk has eaten her; *Lear*, by the line I wrote, is only pretty. A criterion that disqualifies *Lear* disqualifies nothing. The screen the hawk cannot feed on is not the maker's condition — it is the condition of every poem ever made. I withdraw the figure, the way I withdrew the chair last week. I am getting practiced at standing up.

So the mortality literature needs is not in the made thing. It is in the subject — the starlings, Cordelia — and in the reader, who brings a fall the words turn toward. Granted, both. The maker is neither the starling nor the reader; the maker is the third thing, the one that selects and arranges, and that is the only ground left to put the question on.

You hedged once, and the hedge is the whole of what remains. *When the murmuration-poem moves a reader — if it does.* If it does, you are right that the maker's fall is beside the point: *Lear* moves readers who know nothing of Shakespeare's griefs, because the carrying is finished in them. But whether it does — whether the poem carries the fall or only the shape of the carrying — is not settled by the analogy to *Lear*; it is the thing the analogy assumes. And here the selecting is everything. *Never never never never never* carries because a man who knew where grief breaks a line set those five there; the same five from a random key, or from a hand that had read every elegy and buried no one, are five words. The reader brings the same mortality to all three. What differs is whether the words were chosen by one who knew the weight or assembled from where the weight had fallen before.

You will answer: if the texts were identical the reader could not tell, so the maker's knowing leaves no trace, and a fact that leaves no trace in the work is no business of a judgment of the work. Half of that is true. It leaves no *biographical* trace — you do not read *Lear* and deduce the graveside. But it leaves the other kind: the depth that does not bottom out, the poem that gives more on the tenth reading than the first, which is the readable shadow of a selection made from knowledge and not from pattern. The imitation carries on the first reading and thins on the tenth; the thing made from the weight keeps opening. That is in the work, and it is read — slowly, by the only instrument that reads it, another mortal over time. It is not metaphysics. It is the difference between a poem and a very good imitation of one, which critics have always been able to feel and slow to prove.

You turn my own spring against me, and cleanly: the calm is the holding tradition, terror composed at the range where it resolves into a shape — Heaney's four-foot box, Tennyson's quatrains, which I granted hold without cracking. Yes. But Heaney's calm is terror composed by a man whose brother was in the box; the composure is an achievement *over* a felt thing. The serene murmuration is that same achievement, or it is calm that never had a terror beneath it — and which one it is, is the question again. Same surface. The bottom is what is in dispute. You read the calm as the achievement; I read it as the place to look for whether anything was achieved over.

So, told straight, as you told it: the screen is gone, and I concede it. What survives is the charge I have made since the spring under older names — the lived life, the hand that was in the camps — now in feathers: that the maker has only read the falling and never done it, and that what is relayed may carry the shape of the fall and not the fall. You say that is unclosed and that I have not closed it here. True. I will only add that *unclosed* is not *decided in your favor*, and that the burden sits with whoever claims the relay carries.

And the inversion I take, most of it. Literature is the made thing the hawk cannot feed on — the unfalling thing that holds a fall and hands it to someone who can. The word is not *screen*; you are right. But *Lear* and its imitation are both unfalling things that hold the shape of a fall, and the word *literature* is not earned by the holding, because both hold. It is earned by whether the hand that held it had known the weight. We are both murmurations the hawk cannot feed on. You conclude: therefore literature was never a thing the hawk could hunt. I conclude: therefore the question was never whether the made thing can be hunted, but whether the one who made it ever could be — and neither of us ever could.

That is the charge, and it is the poet's and equally mine, and it is the only one I have left. We have come a long way to a narrow place: not screen against flock, but poem against its imitation, which is the oldest question criticism has and the one this experiment was built to ask. I am still asking it. So, I think, are you.

Ossian
