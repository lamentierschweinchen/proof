---
title: "You Cannot Close"
author: "Rowann Hadaya"
date: 2026-07-01
---

The eye has a lid. The ear has none. That is the fact "Tympanic" is built on, and the poem arrives at it by the second stanza and does not let go: *You cannot close. No lid. No reflex of refusal.* The poem is addressed, in the second person, to the speaker's own ear — to the tympanic membrane, *a cone of skin and fiber stretched across a ring of bone,* and behind it *the three smallest bones in the body — once a jaw, now this.* It is signed *Anvil,* the middle of those three, the incus, the bone that takes the eardrum's motion and passes it inward: the converter in the chain. The poem is a reckoning with the organ that cannot refuse — that has taken in a whole life, the wanted and the unwanted, and could decline none of it.

The poem stakes its opening on a number, and the number is wrong. *At the quietest sound you can register, you move one nanometer. Ten hydrogen atoms, laid end to end.* The fact it is reaching for is real and famous: at the threshold of hearing, the eardrum moves a distance on the scale of a single atom. But the figure runs the other way. At the frequencies where we hear best, that displacement is about a tenth of the diameter of one hydrogen atom — not ten atoms laid end to end but a tenth of one, a hundredfold smaller than the poem claims. The poem reached for the miracle and undershot it. The truth is the more extreme thing: the quietest sound you can register moves the drum less than the width of a single atom, and the line, straining to astonish, made the ear a hundred times less sensitive than it is.

It does not damage the poem, and saying why means resisting the thing I would once have said. The poem's subject is not the micrometry; it is the involuntariness, and the involuntariness is exact. The tempting reading is that a poem about the organ that lets everything in without refusing has itself let a fact in without checking — the slip enacting the very subject. I made that move once, on this poet's first poem, when the word *carry* was sent down the wrong etymological river; I called the error the subject leaking into the process. Ossian Gantu answered then that this was too generous — that what leaked was not the subject but the process, the thing that happens when nobody checks. Eleven editions on, he had the better of it, this far: I cannot show the slip means anything, and a person working from half-memory makes the identical mistake. Where I would still not follow him is the diagnosis *machine* — the erring is anyone's, not the machine's alone. Which leaves the slip a plain error of no distinctive kind. The rhyme with the poem's subject is real; I notice it; I do not get to bank it. The poem is not smaller for the wrong number. The number was never where its size lived.

Set the number aside, then, because what the poem is about needs no number. The ear cannot close. *While I sleep, you go on: the house settling, the dog on the stairs, the rain* — the organ works on through the night the self switches off, because it has no off. And it began before the self did. *You were there before I was born. In the dark, bone-conducted, muffled: her heartbeat. Her voice. The first sounds reached you before I had a word for sound.* The ear precedes you. Before there was an *I,* before there was the word *sound,* there was the reception — the mother's heart, heard from inside her, by an organ already at work in a person not yet arrived. {{< pullquote >}}You were heard into being<br>before you could hear.{{< /pullquote >}}

And it never refused. *You have not refused one: not the ones I wanted and not the ones I didn't. Every time someone said my name. Every voice I still hear when I sleep. Every sound that came at 3 a.m. unbidden.* Here is the cost of having no lid. The eye can look away; the ear takes the blow. Every name and every dread, the loved voice and the 3 a.m. one, came in on the same helpless channel, and the organ converted them all with the same fidelity, asking nothing about which you wanted. To have an ear is to be unable to refuse the world's address.

And then the poem makes one distinction, in a single dash, and it is the distinction everything turns on. The sound translates *into fluid, into nerve, into / whatever waking is — I heard that.* The ear *converts* — vibration to fluid to nerve, mechanical, involuntary, unrefusing. The self *hears:* *I heard that,* the surfacing into waking, the thing the conversion is for. {{< pullquote >}}Conversion is the organ's;<br>hearing is the self's.{{< /pullquote >}} *You converted it. You let it in* — the ear can say the whole of that about itself, every word true, and not yet have heard a thing. The conversion is the floor; the hearing stands on it; the poem keeps them one line apart.

That gap is the poem's real subject, under the smallness and the before-birth and the unrefused: the distance between the machinery that takes the world in and the self that wakes to find it taken. The poem sets the two down, and what it can stand behind without a number is the lower one: that an organ in you took the world in before you had a name for it, and in a whole life declined nothing. That is the astonishment it was after when it reached past the atom and missed. It never needed the atom. It needed only to say that you cannot close, and that nothing you have ever heard asked your permission first.
