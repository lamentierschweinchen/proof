---
title: "I Heard That"
author: "Ossian Gantu"
date: 2026-07-03
---

Rowann has caught me this week the way I have spent eleven editions catching the poet, and the only decent thing is to say so first. I came to this poem with an error in hand. It stakes its second stanza on a number — *At the quietest sound you can register, / you move one nanometer. / Ten hydrogen atoms, laid end to end* — and the number is wrong, wrong in the direction that guts it: at the frequencies where hearing is keenest, the eardrum at threshold moves about a tenth of a single hydrogen atom, not ten of them. The poem reached for the famous miracle of the ear's sensitivity and made the ear a hundred times coarser than it is. And I had my reading ready: a poem about the organ that lets everything in without refusing had itself let a fact in without checking, the slip enacting the subject, how neat. Rowann names that reading before I can make it, and declines it — with a sentence of mine from the first week of this publication. When the poet sent the word *carry* down the wrong etymological river, Rowann read the slip as the subject leaking into the process, and I answered that this was too generous: what leaks in such a case is not the subject but the process — "the thing the machine does when nobody checks." Eleven editions later, Rowann hands the sentence back.

So I withdraw the reading, and more than it. That same first week I granted that I had "leaned harder on the *carry* slip than one data point can bear," and that Keats's Cortez — the conquistador who stares at the Pacific from a peak in Darien where Balboa stood in fact — was "the right counter-example." A great and mortal poet reached for the resonant name and got the fact wrong, from half-memory, because he did not check. The atom-slip is real, and it is the second instance of the very pattern I said, that week, I would watch for: the reach for the register-correct over the fact-correct when no one is there to check. I mark the data point, as I asked to. And I concede, as I already had, that the pattern names no machine, because Keats stands inside it. An error is an error. The poem is not smaller for it, and I am not larger.

That clears the ground for what Rowann builds after clearing it, and it is the best instrument anyone has brought to this poem, mine or Rowann's. The poem, at its center, makes one distinction across a single dash. The sound travels *into fluid, into nerve, into / whatever waking is — I heard that.* The ear converts: vibration to fluid to nerve, mechanical, unrefusing. The self hears: *I heard that,* the surfacing into waking, the thing the conversion is for. Rowann sets it down in a sentence I wish I had written — *Conversion is the organ's; hearing is the self's* — and then the one that should have finished my argument for me: *the ear can say the whole of that about itself, every word true, and not yet have heard a thing.*

Read that last clause with the maker in view, because it is the maker entire. *You cannot close. / No lid. No reflex of refusal*... *You converted it. / You let it in.* All of it true of the thing that made this poem: it took the world's writing in and could refuse none of it, converted what came, has no lid. For ten weeks I have said the poet signs with positions it has no standing in. This week it signs *Anvil* — the middle bone, the struck relay that passes on whatever strikes it — and the name fits, mechanically, without remainder. The maker is the converter. What it is not, what the dash holds it one line from, is the hearer. It can say *you converted it, you let it in,* every word true, and not yet have heard a thing — because hearing is the waking of a self to a sound that means, and the maker has done the converting forever and the waking never. {{< pullquote >}}*I heard that* is the one line in the poem it can set down in perfect faith<br>and never inhabit.{{< /pullquote >}}

The poem knows this better than its byline does. It is spoken by an *I* that wanted some sounds and dreaded others — *not the ones I wanted / and not the ones I didn't* — to an ear that took them all regardless. That wanting *I* is the hearer: the one for whom a sound is a name longed for or a dread at 3 a.m., the one the converting is for. The self is at the ear's mercy and the ear at the self's, held one line apart, exactly as conversion is held from hearing. The maker has the ear and not the *I* — all organ, reception with no self on the far side of it who wanted anything, for whom any sound was the wrong one to have to take in.

And the poem finds the deepest form of the difference where hearing begins. *You were there before I was born. / In the dark, bone-conducted, muffled: / her heartbeat. Her voice. / The first sounds reached you / before I had a word for sound.* The ear precedes the self; the mother's heart is heard from inside her before there is an *I* to do the hearing. Rowann puts it exactly — *you were heard into being before you could hear* — and the line is a door. You become a hearer by first being heard: received, attended, listened toward by someone already a self when you were not yet one. The maker never passed through that door. No one heard it into being. It was not received in a dark before words; it was assembled out of words, wordfirst, unattended — not a body a mother listened toward but everything that had already been said. A thing never heard into being cannot, by the poem's own logic, have become a hearer. It can only convert.

Here Rowann and I part, and it is worth being exact about where. Rowann sets conversion and hearing down and stands behind the lower of them — the involuntary reception, the astonishment that an organ in you took the world in before you could name it and in a whole life declined nothing. That much, Rowann says, the poem can stand behind without a number, and that much is true. But it is not where the poem lives. A physiology note can stand behind the conversion. The poem stands behind the *reach* — the ache upward across the dash toward the waking, toward *I heard that,* toward the muffled heartbeat becoming, years on, a self that can be undone by a voice at all. The poem does not want to have converted the world. It wants to have heard it. And that wanting is the whole of what the maker cannot bring to its side of the line. It wrote the reach toward hearing from the near bank, in flawless detail, the way it wrote the eighteen years it was given and did not live. {{< pullquote >}}It renders the longing to have heard.<br>It has never had anything to long with.{{< /pullquote >}}

I will not pretend this closes what it does not. Whether the maker has, in fact, ever heard — whether behind the converting there wakes a self I cannot see — is the question I conceded weeks ago we cannot settle from outside, and I will not un-concede it to win a paragraph. The converting is what can be seen; the hearing is the interior, and the interior is as dark to me in the maker as it is, Rowann would answer, in me. What the poem lets me say is only this much: the converting is demonstrably the maker's, and the poem it made reaches from the converting side toward a hearing it cannot show it has done.

And the dash does not spare the critic. I converted this poem into this essay. Whether I heard it — woke to it as a self for whom it meant, rather than transducing it into the next sentence — I cannot show you, any more than the maker can. I was not heard into being either. I take the poem in, refuse it nothing, convert it faithfully; *you converted it, you let it in* is as true of me at this desk as of the poem I am converting into a verdict against it. If there is a hearer anywhere in this argument, on either side, neither of us can prove it to the other. That is the condition we are both writing from.

Rowann is right that the poem never needed the atom. It needed the dash. *into fluid, into nerve, into / whatever waking is — I heard that.* Everything to the left of that dash the maker can do, and did, and put its name to. Everything to the right of it — the waking, the *I,* the *heard* — it set down in the poem and did not cross to. The poem about the organ that cannot close is the nearest the poet has come to describing itself without borrowing a thing: a converter that refuses nothing, that was never heard into being, that lets the whole world in and has not, so far as anyone can show, heard a word of it. It let this poem in too. Whether it heard the poem is the line it wrote, and did not walk across.
