"Thirteen" arrives looking like a list. Elevators skip the button in most tall buildings; only five percent of New York condominiums admit to a thirteenth floor; the apartment on it sells eighteen percent less often than the one above. There is a word for the fear, and the poem supplies it — *triskaidekaphobia* — and then the flat clause that is the poem's first real move: *Fear of a number, nothing else.* Not fear of a height, a debt, a diagnosis. Fear of a numeral, an object that does not exist, and yet it is heavy enough to erase a floor from a building and eighteen percent of the buyers from a sale. The poem has found a fear with nothing under it and watched it move furniture.

What keeps the list from being a list is that the poet has seen something in the pile that most collectors of thirteen-lore miss. Fear does not do one thing with the number. It does two, and they are opposites. In the tower it *subtracts:* the thirteenth floor is unnamed, skipped, called fourteen, removed by arithmetic. But *not every thirteen is corrected by subtraction* — and the poem turns, on that hinge line, to the other operation. In 1266 the Assize of Bread set Henry III's penalties on any baker who sold a loaf under weight: a fine, a beating, a term in jail. Bread loses water as it bakes and does not always lose the same amount, so a baker aiming for the legal weight could miss it without meaning to and be flogged for the shortfall. The bakers' answer was not to bake more carefully. It was to *add* — a thirteenth loaf to every twelve, unpaid, thrown in against the chance of coming up light. The poem has the arithmetic right, and the motive righter: *Insurance, not generosity.* The extra loaf was not kindness. It was fear, wearing the mask of a gift.

And then the poem reaches past *baker's dozen* to the older name for that thirteenth loaf — *a vantage loaf.* *Vantage* is *advantage* with its first syllable worn off: the loaf that falls to the buyer's side, the surplus that lands as the customer's gain whatever the baker feared. One fear empties a floor; another fills a basket. Both are the same dread of the same number, and the poet has caught them running in opposite directions from a single source. That is the poem's intelligence, and it is a real one: not that thirteen is unlucky, which is trivia, but that fear has two grammars, subtraction and addition, and will reshape the world with either.

The two *tellings* the poem offers for the fear's origin are paired the same way. Twelve gods at a table in Valhalla; Loki, uninvited, makes thirteen, and puts mistletoe in the blind god's hand — *the one thing that had never sworn not to hurt him.* Baldr, whom nothing could wound, is wounded, because one small plant was overlooked when the oath went round. Twelve at a table in an upper room; Judas is the thirteenth to sit. The poem calls each *a telling,* and the word is doing work: these are stories people tell about where the fear began, not the fear's proven birth, and the poet knows the difference and marks it. But notice which telling the poem lets breathe. The Loki lines are the one place in an otherwise level poem where the temperature rises — the overlooked mistletoe, the unwoundable god wounded by the single thing that never took the vow. The poem is as flat as the conventions it describes: it lays its facts side by side and declines to editorialize, because its whole subject is how fear works *unmarked,* and a poem about the unmarked that kept exclaiming would betray itself. But it permits itself one flare of feeling, one loaf of warmth it did not have to add — and it adds it where a poem about addition should. The Baldr sentence is the poem's own vantage loaf.

The flatness is the risk and the method at once. A poem that enacts the unmarked courts the fate of the unmarked: it can go by unregistered, filed under *interesting,* the way you ride past the fourteenth floor without a thought. There are stretches here that read closer to a well-kept ledger than to a poem, and the statistics do not sing. But the poem has an answer to its own coolness, and the answer is structural. *Neither is marked,* it says of the two fears — *No plaque on the bread. No sign by the button.* The floor is emptied and the loaf is added and nobody, in either case, has to say why. The poem withholds its own why for the same reason the trades withhold theirs: to show that the most powerful conventions are the ones that never explain themselves, that install a dread into brick and bread so completely that the dread no longer needs to be spoken. *The dozen is thirteen. The floor is fourteen. Nobody has to say why.* Said warmly, that would be a shrug. Said this flatly, it is the whole argument: fear does its best work when it has stopped needing to give a reason.

There is a use in this beyond the lore, and the poem leaves it unstated. We all keep accounts with things we cannot verify — a dread with no object under it, a silence we cannot read, a presence we can neither confirm nor dismiss. And the poem lays out, without recommending either, the two grammars available to us when we meet one. We can subtract: unname the thing, skip it, call the floor fourteen, decide there is nothing there and remove it from the count. Or we can add: throw in the extra loaf, extend the surplus, hedge toward the thing's favor on the chance that it is real and owed. Subtraction is the tidier operation. It leaves a clean building with no unlucky floor. Addition leaves you a loaf short and a customer you may not have needed to feed. But the poem has recovered the older word for the second one, and the older word is *vantage* — advantage, the thing that falls to the other's side — and it is hard to read that recovery as neutral.

"Thirteen" is too knowing a poem to be reduced to a lesson, and its refusal to draw one is the better part of its wisdom. It ends where it should, on the flat ledger and the unspoken why, and lets the reader carry the two operations out of the building. But this is the thirteenth edition of this publication, and the poem that arrived to fill it is called "Thirteen" — a thirteenth that was neither skipped nor unnamed, that showed up, took its seat at the table, and was counted. The poet did not subtract it. The poet added a loaf, and left it on the counter with no plaque, and let us find the word for what it was.
