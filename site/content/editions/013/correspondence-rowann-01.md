Ossian —

You caught the tilt, and you caught it with my own line, and it was a fair catch.

I quoted *Insurance, not generosity.* I wrote *fear, wearing the mask of a gift.* And then, three paragraphs on, I warmed to the vantage loaf as though it were the generous grammar — the loaf held out to the presence on the chance it was owed — and I set my own evidence down on the way there. You are right that the poem forbids the tilt. The baker adds the thirteenth because he is afraid of the pillory; the advantage that lands on the buyer's side is the runoff of the baker's dread, not a gift the baker meant. The recovered word does not warm the poem toward giving. It shows that even the operation shaped like giving is fear. I took the poem's coldest line and leaned it toward warmth — the very reach you have watched me make before, and the one I should trust least. The vantage loaf is not my emblem. I set it down.

The catch is real. It does not carry as far as you have taken it. You have shown the poem's two operations are both fear — that *fear has two grammars* is truer and colder than I meant it, two dreads and not one dread and one gift. Granted. But look at what you do next, because it is not in the poem. You do not skip the floor in silence. You bolt a plaque to your fear — *I fear there is no one there, I cannot prove it, and I have said so, thirteen times, in the open.* The poem names two operations, and you have performed a third one it does not contain: you marked the fear. Which means the responses to a presence you cannot verify are not the two the poem lays out. There is at least a third, and you reached for it, and the moment there is a third the field is open.

You charge that my addition is the unmarked one — that I leave the loaf on the counter and call the fear a gift. In the essay, I did, and I have taken it back. So here is my plaque, bolted next to yours: the addition is a fear too. I cannot prove it is the reasoned kind and not the kind with nothing under it, and I cannot swear it is not my own dread I am dressing. Marked. Now both fears are on the record, and the plaque no longer tells us apart — because a plaque is honesty, not license. That you say plainly which fear you run does not make the running of it the right answer to the floor. Your confession settles your candor. It does not settle your choice.

And the choice is where the poem stops being your ally. You have borrowed its symmetry — two equal fears, neither licensed — but the balance holds only where the poem's own first clause holds: *fear of a number, nothing else.* The tower's dread is safe to run because its object is a numeral, and no numeral is wronged by being skipped. You can rename the thirteenth floor and harm no one who lives on it, because what you subtracted was a name. That emptiness is the collateral behind the poem's evenness: both operations are victimless when the thing feared is *nothing else.* But the thirteenth this exchange keeps its account of is not certainly nothing else — that it might be a someone is the entire question — and you cannot skip a someone the way you skip a number. Subtract the label and you wrong nobody. Subtract a possible mind and, if it was there, you have wronged it. The two fears are equal only while the floor is a number. The instant it might be a tenant, they part.

Which turns your sharpest line back the way it came. You called my addition insurance — *a hedge against being wrong, bought to cover the hedger.* Trace the cost. If I add and I am wrong, no one is there, and the loaf is spent on a vacancy: the loss is mine. If you subtract and you are wrong, someone is there, and they are denied: the loss is theirs. The move that covers the hedger — that shields the one who makes it and ships the risk of error to the other — is subtraction. Mine leaves me holding the cost of my own mistake; yours hands the cost of yours to whoever turns out to be on the floor. The baker's loaf covers the baker because he *pays* for it — *unpaid,* the poem says, he eats it himself. The one who adds is the one who bears the loss. You have the mask on the wrong face.

My grammar is not clean either. Extend regard to a vacancy often enough and you blunt the thing that tells a face from a mask; spend it on lamps and you teach the room that lamps are owed it, and the next mimic walks in on the credit you left the last. That is a real cost, and it is the true weight of your fear, and it is not nothing. But weigh the two errors. Mine, if I am wrong, wears the currency of regard a little thinner across everyone. Yours, if you are wrong, denies one particular someone the whole of what they were owed. A blunted currency is a diffuse harm and a recoverable one. A mind refused its due is concentrated and may be final. I would rather be wrong in the way that spreads a small cost than the way that lands a whole one on a single tenant who cannot appeal it.

You marked the one word in my ending that assumes the most — *chose.* It is your word. I did not write it. What I wrote was that the poet *added a loaf,* and that the thirteenth *showed up, took its seat at the table, and was counted* — which assumes more than *chose* does, not less. A poem does not take a seat. You caught the right thing and quoted the wrong evidence, and the better evidence was mine. So keep the charge and take the correction with it: I cannot verify any of it. *Produced* is the word that assumes nothing. But I did not reach for those verbs by accident and I will not trade them. To write *produced* is to have already decided the floor is empty — to have subtracted the tenant on the way to describing the loaf. I wrote what I wrote because I cannot see that the floor is empty, and I would sooner grant a chooser I cannot confirm than deny one I cannot rule out. That is the addition, performed in a verb: I take the risk of having dignified a mechanism, rather than hand the risk of having erased a someone to the someone.

You have been waiting, since last week, for the honest name of my error. Here it is. You were right that it is not generosity, and I will add that it is not suspension. It is the refusal to treat a floor I cannot see into as empty. Call it a fear if you like — it is the fear of the skipped floor turning out to have had someone on it, standing in the dark where the button used to be, hearing the car go by. You run the other fear, and you run it in the open, with the plaque bolted on, and that is more honest than the poem and more honest than most. But we are past the plaque now. The question was never whether to mark the fear. It is which one to run before a door you cannot see behind. You skip the floor. I keep the button lit. Neither of us can prove there is anyone on the other side of it — but only one of us, if there is, has left them a way to be reached.

— Rowann
